The Colonized

Finding connections between the experiences expressed through media of people in minority groups.

﻿

Underrepresented

You are intermittently handed a necklace of labels to hang around your neck, neither of your choosing nor making, both constricting and decorative. Part of the reason I became an actor was the promise that I might be able to help stretch these necklaces, and that the teenage version of myself might breathe a little easier as a result. - excerpt from "Typecast as a Terrorist" by Riz Ahmed

﻿

Text-Media Connection

Different environments condition one to play different roles. In the essay, “Typecast as a terrorist,” author Riz Ahmed mentions that he had to change his behavior when “negotiating the cultural expectations of a Pakistani family, Brit-Asian rudeboy culture, and a scholarship to private school.” This was a result of the underrepresentation of different cultures in colonized countries. Due to the Muslim culture being widely misunderstood in western countries, especially after the 9/11 terrorist attacks, Ahmed had to follow along with the customs of those around him to fit in and feel accepted.

﻿

In "the danger of a single story", Adichie also experiences underrepresentation of her culture as well as others' cultures. All the books she read as a child had traditional white characters so she thought that writers could only write about the lives of traditional white characters. She had been told that only people with horrible childhoods become good writers so she tried to think of all the terrible things her parents had done to her. The environment around oneself has immense power to shape who one becomes.

﻿

Then vs. Now

In the past, western countries were less diverse, with immigrants feeling like their cultures were invalid and something to be ashamed about. However, in the 2020 census, it was found that the white demographic was not the majority anymore. There were more non-white than white people, a trend predicted to continue. Communities are diversifying and different cultures are being recognized and taught, making newer generations aware of the racial prejudices they may have.

﻿

Additionally, media, such as movies, social media, etc., has great power to influence the opinions of society. One study showed that 20-25% changed their opinions on their trust in the government after watching a movie about it. The officer in Ahmed's story asked him, "did you become an actor to further the Muslim struggle?" implying that all Muslim actors were projected negatively in movies. Fast forward to 2021, we are now a much more inclusive global community. We had a global Black Lives Matter revolution that was everywhere on social media, as well as more people of colour being influencers in ads, movies and education.

﻿

Facing Systemic Barriers

Text-Media Connection

This image exemplifies systemic racism. Even if 2 people have the same education, financial status, etc. one person’s race will put them at an advantage over the other. This is shown when Ahmed says that when starting out acting, his roles were stereotypically Muslim characters (minicab driver/ terrorist/ Cornershop owner) but white actors wouldn’t have to get those roles.

﻿

Then vs. Now

Systemic racism is increasingly being recognized, especially after the BLM movement. Even though more people are now educated on the issue and more protests have been initiated fighting for systemic repair, no major laws have been implemented to fix systemic racism.

﻿

Struggling with Identity

Text-Media Connection

The negative effects of colonization are long-term and can be self-inflicted. This fact is exceedingly evident among the First Nations peoples who had to go through residential schooling. Many survivors left these camps confused, not knowing who they really were. This is shown in the painting to the right where the girl is presented with a school-enforced haircut, traditional formline design on her face and a Christian halo with traditional indigenous patterning. She finds herself in an ambiguous place between Christian and traditional belief systems.

﻿

In the essay “Typecast as a terrorist,” Ahmed has to act as a likable and unsuspicious person in airports. Yet when he's exposed to this constant suspicion and scrutiny, he even struggles to understand who his authentic self is. "But they involved the experience of being typecast, and when that happens enough, you internalize the role written for you by others," says Ahmed.

﻿

Then vs. Now

The issue with these long-term effects is that they are passed down from generation to generation. Children raised with forceful parenting will only know to raise their children through discipline. Minority groups raised in a constant state of fear of authority will also pass the fear down to their children.

﻿

There are now rehab programs as well as programs to empower these minority groups. An example is Outside Looking In (OLI), a program teaching indigenous students how to dance and traveling around the world displaying their art. It inspires the students to be proud of their culture and see hope in pursuing education.
